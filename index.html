What is a Tardigrade?

Also known as water bears or moss piglets, tardigrades are microscopic animals renowned for their extraordinary survival abilities

Phylum Tardigrada

Tardigrades are microscopic, water-dwelling animals first described by Johann August Ephraim Goeze in 1773. These remarkable eight-legged segmented micro-animals have captivated researchers for centuries due to their unique morphology and extraordinary ability to survive extreme environmental conditions. With over 1,300 described species distributed across terrestrial, freshwater, and marine ecosystems worldwide, tardigrades represent a distinct phylum of ecdysozoans, sharing ancestry with arthropods and nematodes.

Their cuticle, which must be periodically molted during growth, provides protection while maintaining flexibility. The tardigrade body plan consists of a head segment followed by four body segments, each bearing a pair of legs terminated by claws or adhesive discs adapted to their specific ecological niche.

Towards a Whole Connectome

Mapping the complete neural network of tardigrades to understand their sensory processing and behavioral control

Phase 1: High-Resolution Imaging

Using serial block-face scanning electron microscopy (SBF-SEM) and focused ion beam scanning electron microscopy (FIB-SEM) to capture complete volumetric data of tardigrade nervous systems at synaptic resolution.

Phase 2: Neural Reconstruction

Employing advanced image segmentation algorithms and machine learning to trace individual neurons, dendrites, and synaptic connections throughout the entire nervous system.

Phase 3: Functional Mapping

Correlating structural connectivity with behavioral experiments and calcium imaging to understand information processing pathways and neural circuit function.

Phase 4: Comparative Analysis

Comparing connectomes across tardigrade species to identify conserved and derived neural features, illuminating the evolution of nervous system organization in ecdysozoans.

Why Tardigrade Connectomics?

Tardigrades possess a compact yet sophisticated nervous system with approximately 10,000-20,000 neurons, making them an ideal model organism for complete neural mapping. Their evolutionary position as panarthropods provides crucial insights into the origins of complex neural processing, while their extreme survival capabilities may be partially encoded in unique neural adaptations. A complete tardigrade connectome will serve as a foundational resource for understanding invertebrate neurobiology and the neural basis of behavior.
