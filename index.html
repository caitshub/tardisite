What is a Tardigrade?

Also known as water bears or moss piglets, tardigrades are microscopic animals renowned for their extraordinary survival abilities.

Phylum Tardigrada

Tardigrades are microscopic, eight-legged invertebrates that live in diverse environments ranging from deep oceans to damp moss. They are widely known for their ability to enter a dormant state called cryptobiosis, allowing them to survive extreme temperatures, high pressure, radiation, and the vacuum of space. Once reintroduced to water, they rehydrate and return to their normal active state.

Tardigrades possess a segmented body plan consisting of a head and four trunk segments, each supporting a pair of legs. Despite having a relatively small number of cells and a simple nervous system with few neurons, they exhibit complex limb coordination and can adjust their walking gaits based on the surface they are traversing. They also feature rudimentary eyespots, known as ocelli, which allow them to detect light.

Towards a Whole Connectome

Here, we present the first whole-body electron microscopy dataset of an adult Hypsibius exemplaris tardigrade. We aim to reconstruct the complete whole-body connectome by mapping every neuronal connection, as well as neuronal interactions with non-neuronal tissues. This comprehensive structural map provides a foundation for understanding how a simple nervous system coordinates complex physiological and behavioral functions.

Phase 1: High-Resolution Imaging

Using serial block-face scanning electron microscopy (SBF-SEM) and focused ion beam scanning electron microscopy (FIB-SEM) to capture complete volumetric data of tardigrade nervous systems at synaptic resolution.

Phase 2: Neural Reconstruction

Employing advanced image segmentation algorithms and machine learning to trace individual neurons, dendrites, and synaptic connections throughout the entire nervous system.

Phase 3: Functional Mapping

Correlating structural connectivity with behavioral experiments and calcium imaging to understand information processing pathways and neural circuit function.

Phase 4: Comparative Analysis

Comparing connectomes across tardigrade species to identify conserved and derived neural features, illuminating the evolution of nervous system organization in ecdysozoans.

Why Tardigrade Connectomics?

Tardigrades possess a compact yet sophisticated nervous system with approximately 10,000-20,000 neurons, making them an ideal model organism for complete neural mapping. Their evolutionary position as panarthropods provides crucial insights into the origins of complex neural processing, while their extreme survival capabilities may be partially encoded in unique neural adaptations. A complete tardigrade connectome will serve as a foundational resource for understanding invertebrate neurobiology and the neural basis of behavior.

Join the Consortium

We are seeking collaborators to join a global consortium dedicated to reconstructing and proofreading the first whole-body connectome of the tardigrade. The underlying H. exemplaris electron microscopy dataset is fully public, and the resulting connectome will also be released as an open-access resource. We are committed to opening this dataset for broad collaboration, inviting researchers to help map the neuronal pathways and tissue interactions that define tardigrade biology.
